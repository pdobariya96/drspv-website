## The Problem

A Rajkot-based manufacturer of industrial valves and fittings received an Income Tax Scrutiny Notice under Section 143(2) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 for Assessment Year 2022-23. The Assessing Officer had raised a demand of **Rs 8,40,000** based on the following additions:

- **Disallowance of Rs 4,20,000** under Section 40A(3) — cash payments exceeding Rs 10,000 to certain vendors
- **Addition of Rs 2,80,000** under Section 68 — unexplained cash credits in the books
- **Disallowance of Rs 1,40,000** under Section 37(1) — personal expenses claimed as business expenditure

The company's previous tax advisor had filed a basic response to the scrutiny notice but failed to provide adequate documentation. The Assessing Officer had already passed a draft assessment order, and the company faced a payment deadline within 30 days.

The promoter was understandably anxious — Rs 8.4 lakh was a significant sum for a company with annual turnover of approximately Rs 3.5 crore, and the demand carried an additional interest liability under Section 234B and 234C.

## What DRSPV Did

Our qualified CAs took over the case and immediately implemented a structured defence strategy:

**Week 1 — Document Reconstruction:**
- Obtained complete bank statements for the relevant assessment year
- Reconstructed the cash payment trail for the Section 40A(3) disallowance — identifying that 7 out of 9 flagged payments were actually made via account payee cheques (the bank entries had been incorrectly classified in the books)
- Gathered confirmation letters from all vendors whose payments were questioned

**Week 2 — Technical Submissions:**
- Filed a detailed response under Section 68, demonstrating that the alleged "unexplained cash credits" were legitimate trade advances received from two long-standing customers. We provided:
  - Customer confirmation letters on their letterheads
  - GST invoices matching the advance amounts
  - Bank statements showing subsequent adjustment against invoices
- For the Section 37(1) disallowance, we segregated the expenses into genuine business expenditure (client entertainment backed by board resolutions) and Rs 18,000 of personal expenditure that the promoter agreed to reverse

**Week 3 — AO Meeting & Representation:**
- Our CAs personally appeared before the Assessing Officer with a compiled submission file
- Presented a reconciliation statement mapping every questioned transaction to its source document
- Cited relevant case law including **CIT vs. Daulat Ram Rawatmull (1973)** on the burden of proof for Section 68 additions

## The Result

| Parameter | Before DRSPV | After DRSPV |
|---|---|---|
| Section 40A(3) Disallowance | Rs 4,20,000 | **Rs 0** (fully deleted) |
| Section 68 Addition | Rs 2,80,000 | **Rs 0** (fully deleted) |
| Section 37(1) Disallowance | Rs 1,40,000 | **Rs 18,000** (only genuine personal expenses) |
| **Total Demand** | **Rs 8,40,000** | **Rs 18,000** |
| Interest Liability (234B/C) | ~Rs 95,000 | **Rs 2,100** |
| **Effective Savings** | — | **Rs 9,14,900** |

The entire matter was resolved at the assessment stage itself — no appeal was required. The company saved not just the tax demand but also the time, stress, and legal costs of pursuing the matter before CIT(A) or ITAT.

**Time to resolution:** 23 days from engagement to final assessment order.
